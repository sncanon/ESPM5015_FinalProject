What qualifies as an

Invasive Species?

Not all exotic species are invasive.

  • Exotic Species

    As a geographically-based definition, exotic species are also known as nonindigenous species. These species are introduced from another place and considered foreign, alien, or non-native; they are not originating and living or occurring naturally in a particular area or environment.


  • Naturalized

    A non-native species, but established as if it is native.


  • Invasive

    As opposed to a the geographical origin and location used to define exotic, the term invasive refers to behavioral attributes. Invasive referrs to a species' tendancy to spread, infringe, proliferate, and persist to the detriment of the environment or human activity.

    These species are considered a nuicance or harmful and lead to negative effects, including: reducing native species, the leading cause of biodiversity loss, disrupting ecosystem functions, disrupt recreation, increasing costs to business, commerce, and maintenenct/control.


  • Exotic vs. Invasive: Differentiating

    Exotic (nonindigenous) species can be either good or bad. If their establishment is bad, the exotic species is often referred to as invasive or harmful. If the establishment is good, the exotic species is frequently referred to as benign or beneficial.

Lantana camara

Description.

Lantana camara is a small terrestrial flowering shrub that grows from one to four feet tall. The plant forms in dense thickets with the stems being rough and hairy, sometimes with short hooked prickles.

The leaves are simple and oppositely arranged along the stem. The leaf blades are mostly egg-shaped in outline with broad end at base and rough in texture.

The small flowers grow in a dense, nearly flat-topped clustered head. The flowers usually bloom yellow or pink but shift into deep orange and scarlet coloration.

The fruit is also small, black (though purplish-black or bluish-black when mature) and glossy in appearance, reaches 3-6 mm in diameter, and contain 1-2 seeds.

Plant Details

Stem and Leaves

Stems and Leaves of Lantana camara

"The young stems are usually green and square in cross-section (quadrangular). They are rough to the touch, often armed with short recurved prickles, and can sometimes be slightly hairy (puberulent).

The simple leaves are oppositely arranged along the stems. They have toothed margins and a somewhat wrinkled appearance. The leaf blades (2-13 cm long and 1.5-7 cm wide) are mostly egg-shaped in outline with pointed tips. The texture of the leaves is usually quite rough, however, the undersides are usually softly hairy." View Image Source

Lantana camara:

Fruit

Lantana camara Fruit

Lantana camara have small, black "berries" (they are actually drupes). The fruit is glossy in appearance and turn purplish-black or bluish-black when mature, reaching a size of 3-6 mm in diameter. Each fruit contains a single hard and stony seed—2-4 mm long, light brown in color and egg-shaped—at its center. Birds assist with plant dispersal as they eat the fruit and transport seeds. Fast Facts.

Here are a few details about Lantana camara

Scientific Classification

Binomial name: Lantana camara
Kingdom: Plantae
Phylum: Magnoliophyta
Class: Magnoliopsida
Order: Lamiales
Family: Verbenaceae
Genus: Lantana
Species: L. camara

Origin

L. camara is native to Mexico, Central America, the Caribbean, and the American tropics.

How it Spread

In the 17th century, Dutch explorers visiting the Americas brought Lantana camara back to European countries where it was then imported into other countries as an ornimental flower. It began to escape from gardens as seeds were distributed by birds and other animals.

Other Names

L. camara goes by the common name of Lantana. It is also known as the West Indian Lantana, big-sage, wild-sage, red-sage, white-sage, angel lips, and tickberry.

Uses

L. camara has many uses. It is most often planted to embellish gardens. Studies have shown that Lantana leaves display antimicrobial, fungicidal, and insecticidal properties; it has been used in traditional herbal medicines for treating a variety of ailments, including skin itches, rabies, chicken pox, measles, asthma, and ulcers.

Special Aspects

L. camara has an ability to last for a relatively long time without water. It also does not have many pests or diseases. Lantana camara attracts butterflies and birds, making it a frequently used plant in butterfly gardens.

L. camara was the first weed ever targeted for classical biological control at the turn of the century.

Lantana camara

Distribution.

According to reports gathered by the Invasive Species Compendium, Lantana camara has become an invasive nuicance in nearly every country it has been introduced to (often as an ornimental plant). The plant does well at establishing and becoming naturalized, particularly in pasture land. Currenly, the plant is noted to be established in over 60 countries worldwide. In regions not highlighted, the plant is often present, but simply regarded as a weed rather than an invasive species.

"The rapid spread of L. camara throughout the tropics is associated with human-induced disturbances. It forms extensive, dense and impenetrable thickets in forestry plantations, orchards, pasture land, waste land and in natural areas. L. camara thrives in open and disturbed areas as well as in open natural vegetation...[and] grows under a wide range of climatic conditions. It has a low tolerance for boggy and saline soils but grows well on poor soils" (Invasive Species Compendium).


Pathways.

L. camara spreads into new countries mainly via the horticultural trade, which spreads numerous varieties of the species. Once introduced into an area, the seeds of L. camara are further dispersed into new areas by birds, sheep, goats, and other animals. Stem fragments or pieces of the rootstock can also give rise to new plants.

So what's the

Problem?

L. camara has a negative impact economically, environmentally, and socially in many locations.

Economic

Records have shown that countries have spent up to $2.2 billion on control for L. camara. As it takes over pastures and crop land, landholders also face loss in product production as L. camara chokes out crops, prevents access to their land and animals, and increases fire hazards. When ingested by cattle and sheep, it may cause photosensitive reactions, diarrhoea, jaundice, hepatitis, and poisoning, leading to further loss for the landowner.

Environmental

L. camara is highly adaptable and quickly displaces native vegatation while it absorbs soil nutrients needed by other plants. The fallen leaves produce allelopathic substances that prevent other plants germinating and growing beneath. This leads to drastic changes in the ecosystem.

Social

In Tanzania and Uganda, L. camara is considered a serious health hazard as its thickets provide breeding grounds for tsetse flies, vectors of trypanosomiasis. The plant also provides potential breeding places for rats, wild pigs, insect pests and plant diseases. There have also been a few cases where children have died after eating unripe berries.

Positives

L. camara is widely regarded as detrimental, however, there are a few notied positive sides. Timorese farmers have considered the plant as highly beneficial as it enhanced soil fertility and soil conditioning. The dense thickets are also known to provide habitat and refuge for wildlife. On top of that, many rural areas are grateful for the extra firewood.

Is there a

Solution?

Being so widespread and resiliant, L. camara is a challenge to control.

  • Mechanical Control

    Pulling, slashing, uprooting, and burning have all been effective mechanical control methods. However, it requires continual follow-up treatment to remove roots and seedlings, plus it is expensive and labour-intensive. It is also shown to only be effective in the short term. Unfortunately, since L. camara is poisonous to livestock, species can not be controlled using large herbivores such as goats and donkeys.


  • Chemical Control

    Experience has shown that chemical control was cheaper and caused less disturbance resulting in higher biodiversity than mechanical control. The most effective herbicides belong to the phenoxy acid (2,4-D, dichloroprop and MCPA), benzoic acid (dicamba) and pyridine groups. Photosynthetic herbicides (triazine and urea) are not effective and the plant is resistant to triclopyr, a widely used herbicide for woody weed control. Factors to consider for treatment include: plant size, time of application, mode of application, and the use of surfactant.


  • Biological Control

    Worldwide, well over 200 releases of biocontrol agents have been made. In the majority of cases, the control agent either failed to become established or became established without achieving control. Yet, this method is considered to be the only viable, long-term control option, since it offers a safe, economic and environmentally benign method of suppressing the weed (CABI).

    Across the globe, Uroplata girardi has been the most successful control agent. In island regions, such as Hawaii, Neogalea sunia and Epinotia lantanae have been successful contributors for control. In Africa, Teleonemia scrupulosa is one of the top biological controls.

    On top of the insect releases, a fungi, Mycovellosiella lantanae, and a rust, Prospodium tuberculatum, have also been used to target L. camara. Both of these infect leaves, petioles, and stems, and can cause systemic infections that lead to stem dieback and defoliation.

A note from the page author

My Story

Read about my first-hand experience with L. camara.

Over the summer of 2016, I had the privilage to intern with the African Lion and Environmental Research Trust in Victoria Falls, Zimbabwe. Southern Africa is a region heavily impacted by L. camara, and I saw it's effects first-hand.

Every Wednesday, I went out to the rural homesteads of Zimbabwe with the research team. Here, I witnessed the effects of L. camara on farmland. Many of the landowners had cattle die due to illness from the plant. Often times, their cropland was taken over with this nuisance shrub, forcing them to turn to labor-intensive work simply to maintain control. A few landowners were greatful for the thorny thickets, as they were able to line their corrals with the branches to keep out predators, such as lions.

Twice a week, a group of volunteers ventured into park areas for manual removal of L. camara (as seen in the gallery video). We would hack at the thickets with axes, uproot them with shovels, and discard them in a pile to burn. The numbers present were overwhelming; a full-day's work barely left a dent. Yet, we pursued forward, knowing this plant was damaging the local ecosystem as it stole nutrients from native species, overtook the land, provided no benefit to indiginous animals, and did not belong in Africa in the first place.

Next to Victoria Falls—an environment similar to L. camara's region of origin—the thickets crowded the edges of the cliffs. This not only distupts the view for tourists (a major economic income for Zimbabwe), but also overpowers native vegetation. In the dry season, L. camara becomes a tangled mess of brown branches, which also took away from the natural beauty of the rainforest-like surroundings of the falls.

These are just a few of the many impacts of L. camara that I witnessed, but the shrub has had many other detrimental roles in other parts of the world, including Hawaii and Australia.

New Information?

Future Research

Lantana camara has been an is a major invasive species worldwide. We know a lot about it, but how can we move forward? I would like to propose a new perspective.

Many areas with Lantana camara present consider the plant to be a mere weed (such as northern North America and European counries). In order to prevent further establishment and invasion, should areas that currently consider L. camara to be a weed change their mindsets and regard L. camara as an invasive? Will this change of perspective assist with worldwide control? How will it impact prevention?

How can I help?

Help stop the spread of Lantana camara and get involved! There are many organizations (such as ALERT) that will take your servive to assist with mechanical control. You can also help by not buying Lantana camara for your own garden. Finally, continue to raise awareness about invasive species!
